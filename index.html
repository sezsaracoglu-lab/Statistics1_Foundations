Statistics 1 — Foundations
✍️ A note from your professor

Welcome back, Sezen. This is your Statistics 1 foundation — the ground floor that everything in Statistics 2 is built on, and yes, your teacher can and will ask about it. It's your Bachelor-1 material re-summarised (Moore, McCabe & Craig Chs. 1, 3–7 and Passer Chs. 1, 2, 4), covering everything EXCEPT correlation. Read it the way you'd walk through a house you already lived in: quickly at first to remember the layout, then slowly through the rooms you'd forgotten. One idea threads the whole course — we use a small SAMPLE to reason about a big POPULATION, and every tool here (a graph, a mean, a probability, a confidence interval, a p-value) is a way to do that honestly while admitting how uncertain we are. Look at each diagram first and let it tell the story, then read the five bullets as captions, then lock it in with the memory hook. Open a theme for its Key Terms table and a must-know checklist. Get this floor solid and Statistics 2 stops feeling like a new building — it's just the next storey up.

Learning Objectives
  • Classify variables (nominal, ordinal, interval, ratio) and choose the right graph for each.
  • Summarise a distribution by shape, centre (mean/median) and spread (IQR, standard deviation), and flag outliers.
  • Read density curves and the Normal distribution, apply the 68–95–99.7 rule, and standardise with z-scores.
  • Distinguish observational from experimental data, state the three causal criteria, and describe randomisation, blocking, and between/within designs.
  • Pick a sampling method (SRS, stratified, multistage) and name the biases that threaten a sample.
  • Apply the rules of probability — addition, complement, multiplication, conditional — and use tree diagrams.
  • Work with random variables: means (and the law of large numbers), variances, and the rules for combining them.
  • Explain sampling distributions, bias vs variability, the Central Limit Theorem, and the binomial distribution.
  • Build a confidence interval and run a significance test (z and t), interpreting p-values, power, and Type I/II errors.
  • Compare two means with two-sample and pooled t-procedures, and know when each applies.
Foundational Terms — the cross-cutting vocabulary

These span the whole course. Each theme also has its OWN Key Terms table — open a theme in the Lessons tab to see it.

TermWorking definition
Population vs SampleThe whole group you care about vs the subset you actually measure.
Parameter vs StatisticA fixed, unknown number describing the population (μ, p) vs its estimate from the sample (x̄, p̂).
SRSSimple Random Sample — every group of n individuals had an equal chance of selection.
DistributionThe pattern of values a variable takes — described by shape, centre, and spread.
Standard deviation (σ, s)The typical distance of values from the mean; the square root of the variance.
Normal distribution N(μ,σ)A symmetric bell curve fixed by its mean and SD; obeys the 68–95–99.7 rule.
z-scoreA standardised value, z = (x − μ)/σ, letting you compare across different distributions.
ProbabilityThe long-run proportion of times an outcome occurs; a number between 0 and 1.
Sampling distributionThe distribution of a statistic across all samples of size n — the engine of inference.
Confidence intervalestimate ± margin of error — a range of plausible values for a parameter.
p-valueThe probability of data at least this extreme if H₀ were true; small p = evidence against H₀.
Central Limit TheoremFor large n, the sampling distribution of the mean is approximately Normal, whatever the population shape.

Synthesis — the thread through Statistics 1

Statistics 1 is one continuous argument about how to speak truthfully about a whole population when you can only ever see a sample of it. It begins by learning to SEE data — classifying variables, drawing the right graph, and pinning a distribution down by its shape, centre and spread — then idealises those pictures into density curves and the Normal distribution, where the z-score turns any value into a universal, comparable number. Next it asks where good data COMES FROM: sound design (randomisation, blocking, between/within, counterbalancing) and sound sampling (SRS, stratified, multistage) are what license causal and general claims, while voluntary-response, undercoverage and non-response quietly destroy them. Probability then supplies the mathematics of uncertainty — rules, conditional reasoning, random variables and their means and variances — which the sampling distribution and the Central Limit Theorem convert into the two great tools of inference: the confidence interval (a range of plausible values) and the significance test (weighing evidence against a null hypothesis, with its p-value, power, and Type I/II errors). Finally the t-distribution lets us do all of this when σ is unknown, and the two-sample and pooled t-procedures let us compare two groups. Every later technique in Statistics 2 — proportions, chi-square, regression, ANOVA — is just this same sample-to-population logic wearing a heavier coat.
